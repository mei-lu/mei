## My Study Plan

Since I work full time and take graduate classes, I needed a fictionless way to get all of this knowledge in my head. So instead of making my own flashcard deck using Anki, as the language community loves to do, I decided to just do a $5 a month subscription on Bunpro. I really liked that the app lets you fill in the blanks instead of just answering if you answered correctly or not.

Since there is a progress bar on the app, I just follow the curriculum without too much pain and stay consistent with my learning. My timeline is to get to N5 level in one year.

At the start, I could learn:

-   10 words a day
-   5 grammar concepts
-   ~150-200 reviews

It got harder and more resistence came as the months went on. The review amounts were also getting overwhelming. In order to continue, I scaled back a bit. There were a few cards that I just couldn't nail down so I wanted to solidify that knowledge first.

My scaled back routine became:

-   5 new vocabulary words
-   2 grammar concepts
-   ~30-50 reviews

## Learning Journey

Japanese has 3 writing systems: hiragana, katakana, and kanji. Kanji are the Chinese characters. I don't think my knowledge of Chinese helped comprehension at N5 level because there isn't much kanji on the exam. It's actually pretty tedious to read pure hiragana or katakana.

As expected, it was easy to learn a bunch of vocabulary. Grammar was always my least favorite part. I much prefer for grammar to come to me intuitively but I don't have that luxury when preparing for an exam. I felt like I learned it begrundgingly as there is no pressure for me to make sensible output yet. I was more motivated by piecing what I knew together and comprehending that way, rather than memorizing strict rules.

I also took every practice exam on the Bunpro app, as well as other ones I found on RedNote and Chinese apps. I probably took 6-7 full exams as practice. When it comes to resources for studying, searching on RedNote always gets the job done. I am really glad to know mandarin and look for those resources! For listening, I relied on Youtube for practice questions, but I wish I practiced it more alongside learning the content.

## Registration

In my experience the signups don't get very full until after a few hours of opening. It's not the same chaotic experience as queuing for Tickermaster, so no worries there. It wasn't very stressful at all. I paid the $100 fee and it definitely gave me a second motivation boost!

I feel like 1 month before the actual exam, I studied pretty lightly actually, and didn't cram. That's refreshing for a change, but at that point I felt like grinding cards I had already seen was getting pretty dry.

## Exam Day

The testing locations are usually universities (around me) and I simply brought 2 pencils, a eraser, and my printed test voucher. It feels very Japanese to have to bring something on paper, but I probably feel that way just because I haven't taken a test with strangers before so we can't simply show student IDs.

We had to seal our phones away in a plastic bag and we were warned that we will be disqualified if any personal devices beep. There were a lot of bubbles to fill out on the front of each test sheet with your information. I definitely double checked those too, since I would be so sad if my test got lost.

I felt a lot of confidence on the vocabulary section, a little less on the grammar (they asked me how to describe units of tea?), and terrible on the listening section. I can feel the collective panic during it because you get to hear the audio only once and you have to make a best guess. Obviously, we all ranted to each other about the listening section after the fact.

I was lucky to have 2 warm and friendly desk partners to chat with in between breaks. We still message over Discord from time to time...what a rare and precious thing to make offline friends organically based off of a common interest.

## N4 Plans

I think I will hold off on N4 until 2027 after I finish my masters degree. We'll see how I feel then. N4 has a lot more content and I don't think it would be wise of me to attempt it until I have more bandwidth. I can already hear some language comprehension nuances that do not make it into subtitles when watching anime, and some punny names of products. That's a lot of the joy of language learning to me.

## Update: I passed!

Yay! I got it in the mail. I felt pretty confident that I had passed, and I'm looking forward to buying a frame eventually.
