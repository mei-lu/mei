## Motivation to Attend

I had been making some VR projects in Unity and Meta Quest at my school's XR club. I had made some buddies along the way and wanted to tag along to see the state of the art.

## Ideation

We had a team of 4, and the operation was really to divide and conquerer. Snap Spectacles is not a product released to the general public (only developer preview) so we had to wing it with 2 days of development time. We started with 3 ideas:

1. Making language flashcards with real life objects
2. Redacting people's faces while recording video
3. Puzzle game to solve quests with tools drawn in the air

Thankfully the mentors at Snap did work with us a lot to hear the pitches. The language one was immediately shot down as they have seen the idea a bunch of times already. I think I was still in my language learning obsession mode as I had just taken a Japanese exam. We made 2 very very MVPs (as low level as you can get at a hackathon) and decided on the puzzle game.

## Development

There was a high school group that came to the event and I was really delighted to demo our project.

I also realized the glasses do not recognize darker skin toned hands as well due to the contrast with the background, which is not surprising but a bummer. I couldn't fix that on the fly but I wished I could have done something to make it easier to use.

## The Pitch

Communication is all that matters at the end of the event. I wanted to demo it really well to the judges so our hard work would not go to waste!

We eventualy developed an elevator pitch to say while the device gets put on the face. We rely a lot on communicating with the judge to guide them on playing the demo, as we cannot see the screen with them (wish we had time to set up a stream). It was really nice to see the delight on their glasses covered faces as what they drew was correctly identified. Or, for less doodle-inclined judges, it was funny to have a misclassification for a "bad drawing".

I knew the demo was important but it gets put on the backburner at first because there is such a fire to want to develop a solid project. It's all technical execution at first and then a scramble to communicate it well.

## Workshops

The first workshop I attended was led by Oliver Awat, a dev and designer from Petricore Games. Aside from the showcase of features we can start with on Meta Quest 3, I actually had a surprising takeaway. I can really feel the relief the studio had when they secured partnerships and funding from companies like Meta. I'm really inspired by their resilliance as a team and company to create their own thing and march on!

The rest were product introductions and guides on how to get started developing with the hardware. Since the products are so new, there's not much prerequisite specific knowledge that you can bring in. I went there to catch a vibe, know what is possible with the hardware, and meet new people. I always expect to learn the most by getting my hands dirty, so just existing in a workshop and soak what I can in was great. I treated it almost like a primer for what I want to do.

## Products

I got to see the Snap Spectacles, RayNeo, Meta Raybans, and Raven Resonance glasses. Spectacles and Ravens were interesting in particular because they haven't been released yet. I had never seen a swappable battery like what the Ravens have.

Meta Raybans have the highest visibility in the market, but after attending a pre-hackathon workshop hosted by Meta, I did not feel inclined to develop with them yet as the SDK did not support using the display. That was a dealbreaker for me.

Snap Spectacles has a lot of developer support, from Lens Studio, the docs, Discord, sample projects, and contests being run. It felt like there is a community being built. The Spectacles kind of have a built in fun factor from the game demos on the glasses to Snap's reputation as company making social products. I also met one of our team members Catherine at the demo table, so I set my heart on that for the hardware.

## Conclusion

I purposefully tried to not have many expectations other than to do my best and learn something at the event. I felt like I met some cool people and it was my first time seeing unreleased products! These events are one of the only times where asking for LinkedIn isn't awkward, and it's great to always approach networking from a place of friendship and have it grow organically. I was really tired as expected but it was rewarding to be scrappy and achieve something in 2 days on a brand new hardware. It was almost like a charcuterie board where I took away a bit of everything.
